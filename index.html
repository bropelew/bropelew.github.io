In Major League Baseball, the shift recently made news as the MLB announced it will be banned starting in the 2023 season. Typically when playing defense in baseball, each team has two infielders to the left of second base (third baseman and shortstop) and two infielders to the right of second base (second baseman and first baseman). But in recent years teams began to utilize shifts, bringing three or even four defenders to one side of second base. Teams were able to look at where the hitters they are facing typically hit the ball, and position their defense in the best spots to take away as many hits as possible. While many people viewed this as strategy and a new pa]rt of Major League baseball, the MLB evidently disagreed as they are bringing forward a ban that will force teams to keep two defenders on each side of second base. The main question that we will be focused on answering is how effective was the shift in taking away hits? Additionally we will look at how usage of the shift has changed over time, which teams are best at using the shift along with some other interesting analysis.

The data that we will be using is from FanGraphs. FanGraphs is a massive database that has information on every baseball stat immaginable. The three main data frames that we be using using are shiftIP (data when the shift was active from 2012-2022), allIP (data from when the shift was both active and not active from 2012-2022), and lastly noShiftIP (data from when the shift was not active from 2012-2022). The years 2012-2022 were chosen as the shift was not used very frequently before the last 10 years.

An extremely important stat that we will be using throughout this project is BABIP. BABIP stands for Batting Average on Balls In Play. This metric is very useful for anaylizing the shift as the shift can only affect balls that are hit in play (the shift has no effect on events such as strike outs or homeruns). By anaylizing how the league average BABIP has changed over time, we should be able to see a relationship between the increasing use of the shift.

This graph, does seem to show that as shifts become more and more frequent, BABIP has decreased, especially in recent years. But if you take a look at the Y axis, you can see that it has not changed by much. From the peak in 2016 to the low in 2022, BABIP appears to have only decreased by roughly .010 points. This means that a batter who hit a ball in play in 2022 got out just about 1% more than a batter who hit a ball in play in 2016. All in all this number does not seem very significant.

An expected BABIP value of 0 means that when shifted, the teams BABIP was the exact same was when not shifted. A positive value of Expected BABIP means that when shifted a team held opposing teams to a lower BABIP than when not shifted, showing the shift worked. For example in this graph the Astros (HOU) are listed at .06. this means that when their defense was in a shift, the opposing team's BABIP was 6% lower than when they were not in a shift.
